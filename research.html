Declines in conifer forest recovery and forest loss from four decades of wildfire in California

Which forest types in California are recovering and which are not?

In this project my co-authors and I used Landsat seasonal surface reflectance time series and machine learning to develop maps of vegetation type and track how the recovery of two different forest types in California has changed from 1985-2022. We used a machine learning approach, a base map of vegetation type (Existing Vegetation Type) from LANDFIRE, and nearly four decades of Landsat seasonal spectra to produce land cover maps and track vegetation cover change.

Spatial patterns of forest recovery across California burn areas.
>0 is cover gain from 1985 to 2022, <0 is loss, =0 is no change

From these maps we tracked the recovery of forest extent for conifer and hardwood forests. We found that 15 years after fire, proportionally for areas burned, conifer forests are recovering extent slower than hardwood forests. We also found that there is a net loss of these two forest types, with much of their cover being replaced by shrubs.

Top row (a-c) are recovery curves for pixels burned the respective burn year bins.
Bottom (d) is a recovery cross section for 15 years since fire. Interpreted as "For burned areas 15 years after fire, vegetation type X is Y% of that to unburned conditions. "

Values greater than 100 indicate burned areas that have more of the given vegetation cover than unbured conditions.

Although the transition to shrubs is a natural process in ecological post-fire succession, the decreasing recovery rate for conifer forests suggests a cause for concern for the forest type. Given continuation of particularly severe wildfire, particularly those with large homogeneous patches of high severity, and climate conditions less suitable for reestablishment, conifer forests in particular may be at risk of continued decline.
